SunScope: An application for forecasting yield in Crop Sunflower under climate change

April 26, 2023

The code for this project can be found on my GitHub

Link to GitHub Repository

The prototype version of the SunScope app can be found here: https://sammajumder.shinyapps.io/SunScope/?_ga=2.11639037.893216896.1682461636-683921061.1681565342

This study also resulted in a scientific paper that is currently under peer-review. The paper can be read for free here: doi: https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.04.07.536012

BACKGROUND: The global food production is vulnerable to adverse effects caused by a changing climate, leading to potential crop losses and affecting food security. Accurate forecasting of crop yield and failure is crucial for farmers and policymakers to evaluate farming practices and plan for future food availability. Machine learning (ML) approaches have shown success in predicting crop yields by handling complex nonlinear data. ML models have also been used to predict crop loss due to diseases and drought. In this study, the focus is on predicting crop yield for the oilseed variety of sunflower (Helianthus annuus) in the United States in 185 counties for the next 60 years under climate change.

QUESTIONS: The project aims to identify global and local patterns that influence sunflower yield using interpretable machine learning approaches. Key questions to be answered include:

  1. What are the specific climate and agricultural practices that have influenced overall sunflower yield across seven states in the contiguous United States? (National Level patterns influencing yield)

  2. Do these factors influence seed yield at an individual state level, or are there state-specific climate and agricultural factors? (Local patterns influencing yields)

  3. Do we expect to see a decline of yield at a county level and how certain are we with our predicitons?

METHODS: The project utilized data from publicly available data sources such as USDA, NOAA and WorldClim to gather yearly seed yield data, area planted and harvested, historical climate data, drought data (1976 upto 2022) and future weather projections (2021 upto 2080). Machine learning algorithms such as Random Forest, Gradient Boosting Machine, and XgBoost are employed for feature selection and forecasting at both the national and individual state levels (National models and State Level models). Recursive Feature Elimination is used to identify influential predictors, and ALE plots are used to elucidate the relationships between predictors and yield.

Links to specific data sources are,

Historical Yield, Agricultural Practices: https://www.nass.usda.gov/Quick_Stats/ Historical Monthly precipitation maximum and minimum temperature: https://www.ncei.noaa.gov/data/nclimgrid-monthly/access/ Historical Drought data: https://www.ncei.noaa.gov/pub/data/nidis/indices/nclimgrid-monthly/ Future Climate data from climate model ACCESS-ESM1-5: https://www.worldclim.org/data/cmip6/cmip6_clim2.5m.html

KEY RESULTS:

  1. At the national level, maximum temperature and precipitation in July (growing season) as well as minimum temperature in September are amongst the most important factors that influence yield.
  1. An increase of precipitation in July beyond 100mm leads to a decline or stagnation of yield. The optimal maximum temperature during July seems to be between 23 to 28 degree Celsius, whereas minimum temperatures between 5 and 10 degree Celsius in May and September seem to positively impact the yield.
  1. The metric used to ascertain model performance was root mean square error (RMSE) on the unseen test dataset. State level models from Nebraska, Texas and Colorado, outperformed National models in terms of yield prediction for the respective states. Normalized RMSE for National model was 0.22 and those for the Colorado, Texas and Nebraska models were 0.17, 0.15 and 0.18 respectively. Pearsons correlations between predicted and actual yield values in the test datasets for these states were R = 0.49, R = 0.35 and R = 0.25 respectively. In North and South Dakota where the national model outperformed the state models for those states. Normalized RMSE for North and South Dakota were 0.30 and 0.27 respectively
