A Machine Learning approach to study plant functional trait divergence

BACKGROUND: Plants diversify across multiple trait axes and this is a hallmark of plant evolution. Some of these traits relate to the fitness of a species and also sheds light on the life history strategies. These traits are known as functional traits amongst ecologists and plant physiologists and tells us about how plants evolve and interact with their environment. These traits are also used by scientists to compare between different plant species and learn about the different ecological strategies that different plant species employ to sustain their respective populations. Identifying these traits in an objective manner is essential to understand how plant species grow, survive, interact with the environment and can be use to model future vegetation dynamics for a given region of the world.

QUESTIONS: This project aimed to identify the traits that contribute most to interspecific phenotypic divergence in multivariate trait space for the genus Helianthus.

DATA:

The data used for this project was sourced from four different publications (Mason and Donovan 2015; Mason et al., 2016; Mason, Goolsby et al., 2017; Mason, Patel et al., 2017) and combined into a single dataset. These publications are The relevant trait data was obtained from a common garden experiment containing 28 diploid wild Helianthus species that were grown under high-resource greenhouse conditions. Each species was represented by 2-4 unique seed accessions, derived from populations across the range of each species, with approximately 5-8 individual plants per population. The combined dataset included a variety of traits such as leaf morphological, ecophysiological, and defensive chemistry traits, whole-plant growth, biomass allocation, phenology traits, as well as floral morphological and ecophysiological traits. The dataset covered over 80% of all diploid nonhybrid species within the Helianthus genus, distributed across the diploid backbone of the Helianthus phylogeny.

METHODS: The study utilized descriptive and predictive machine learning approaches such as Recursive Feature Elimination, the Boruta algorithm, Random Forest, and Gradient Boosting Machine classifiers to analyze trait data. These approaches were applied at the genus level as well as within each of the three major evolutionary clades within the genus to examine the variability in major axes of trait divergence in three independent species radiations. The dataset was divided into a training and a test dataset (80% training and 20% test). Feature selection methods were used on the training dataset as well subsequent model training. Mean Decrease of Gini Impurity was used within Random Forest to determine the relative importance of all traits to species divergence and was used to rank them. Recursive Feature Elimination (metric used was Mean Decrease of Accuracy and algorithm used was Random Forest) was then used to filter down this list to the optimal subset of relevant traits and finally the Boruta algorithm was used to determine the strongly divergent traits in the dataset.

Finally, two classifiers were trained in parallel, a Random Forest and a Gradient Boosting Machine, on the training dataset and validated on the test dataset. This was done to ascertain the validity of the findings of the aforementioned feature selection methods (Recursive Feature Elimination and Boruta).

KEY RESULTS:

The key results of the study showed that machine learning models were able to predict species identity from functional traits with high accuracy, and differences in functional trait importance were observed between the genus level and clade levels indicating different axes of phenotypic divergence. The approach used in this study can provide insights into the predictability or repeatability of evolution through comparison of parallel diversification of clades within a genus.

An in-depth description of the results can be found in accompanying Shiny app: https://sammajumder.shinyapps.io/TraitDivergenceSunflowers/?_ga=2.257923650.893216896.1682461636-683921061.1681565342

The utility of this study is that it demonstrates the potential for machine learning approaches to be implemented in a range of contexts across basic and applied plant science from interspecific divergence to intraspecific variation across time, space, and environmental conditions. The impact of this study could be significant in advancing our understanding of plant functional traits and their relationship to ecological strategies, particularly in identifying important traits that may be relevant for crop breeding, conservation, and management.
